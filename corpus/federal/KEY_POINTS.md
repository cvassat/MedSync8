# Federal Telemedicine Controlled Substance Prescribing — Key Points

Curated summary of the most frequently-cited federal requirements for
telepsychiatry controlled-substance prescribing. Cite the underlying statutes
(`21 USC 829`, `21 CFR 1300.04`) in final policy documents — this file is a
retrieval aid, not a legal source.

## Ryan Haight Online Pharmacy Consumer Protection Act (21 USC 829(e))

**Core requirement.** A prescription for a controlled substance issued by means
of the internet is not valid unless the prescribing practitioner has conducted
at least one in-person medical evaluation of the patient, OR the prescription
is issued in the "practice of telemedicine" as defined in 21 USC 802(54).

**In-person requirement.** The practitioner must personally perform the
in-person evaluation, in the physical presence of the patient, before
prescribing a controlled substance via the internet — unless a telemedicine
exception applies.

**Internet prescribing defined.** Includes any prescription issued by a
practitioner who conducts the medical evaluation via telecommunications
(telephone, video, etc.) without having previously conducted an in-person
evaluation of the patient.

## 21 USC 802(54) — Practice of Telemedicine Exceptions

A controlled substance may be prescribed without a prior in-person evaluation
only in these circumstances:

1. **Hospital/clinic registration.** The patient is being treated by, and
   physically located in, a hospital or clinic registered with the DEA.
2. **Referring practitioner.** The patient is being treated by, and in the
   physical presence of, a DEA-registered practitioner (a "referring
   practitioner").
3. **Indian Health Service / tribal.** Treatment is provided by an IHS or
   tribal organization practitioner.
4. **Public health emergency.** A declared public health emergency triggers
   temporary flexibility (such as the COVID-19 PHE — see 2020–2024 DEA rules).
5. **Special registration.** The practitioner has obtained a special DEA
   telemedicine registration (regulation pending full implementation).
6. **VA providers.** Veterans Affairs health care provider exception.
7. **Other circumstances.** As designated by the Attorney General in
   regulations.

## COVID-19 Public Health Emergency (2020–2025)

During the PHE and its subsequent extensions, DEA permitted controlled
substance prescribing via telemedicine without a prior in-person visit,
subject to the practitioner acting in good faith and in accordance with
applicable state law. This flexibility has been extended multiple times via
temporary rules — verify the current expiration date and any superseding
final rule before relying on it.

## Schedule II Specific Considerations

Schedule II controlled substances (including stimulants used for ADHD:
methylphenidate, amphetamine salts) have received additional scrutiny:

- Some DEA-proposed rules have considered limiting initial telemedicine
  prescribing of Schedule II to a 30-day supply, requiring in-person
  follow-up before continuation.
- State medical boards (notably Texas, California) may impose requirements
  stricter than federal law; check state rules before Schedule II telemedicine
  prescribing.

## PDMP / PMP Requirements

Federal law does not mandate PDMP checks, but most states do. Common state
requirements include:

- **Texas:** Prescribers must check the Texas PMP before prescribing
  opioids, benzodiazepines, barbiturates, or carisoprodol.
- **North Carolina:** NC CSRS check required for initial Schedule II-V
  prescriptions and periodically for continued therapy.
- Check your state medical board's current PMP mandate separately — these
  rules change frequently.

## Documentation Standards

Defensible telemedicine controlled substance documentation should include:

1. Identity verification (government ID check, address confirmation).
2. Informed consent specific to telemedicine.
3. Medical necessity for the controlled substance.
4. Risk-benefit discussion, including risks specific to the substance class.
5. PDMP check result (when applicable by state).
6. Continuity of care plan (referring practitioner, backup provider).
7. Agreement on prescription limits, refill policy, and lost-prescription rules.
